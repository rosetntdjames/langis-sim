LANGIS stands for Likelihood Analysis of National Growth Instability Simulator — and it is also the Filipino word for oil. Both meanings are intentional. Right now, oil is quietly making life harder for every Filipino. The war in the Middle East has pushed oil prices up by nearly 60% since it began — and because the Philippines buys almost all of its oil from that same region, every price increase over there becomes a price increase at every Petron and Shell station here. Diesel is now over ₱104 per liter. Jeepney fares go up. Electricity bills go up. The price of rice, vegetables, and goods in your local palengke — all go up, because trucking them there now costs more.

This app asks one question: how bad could this get, and how likely is it to get that bad?

It doesn't give you a single answer like "the economy will collapse in August." That would be a prediction — and nobody can predict the future. Instead, it gives you a risk level: out of 10,000 possible futures, how many end in serious economic trouble? Think of it like a weather forecast. The forecast doesn't tell you it will rain. It tells you there's a 70% chance of rain — so you decide whether to bring an umbrella. LANGIS does the same thing, but for the Philippine economy.

How does it actually work?

01 — Imagine you want to know: "If I roll a die 10,000 times, how often will I roll a six?" You could calculate it mathematically — or you could just roll the die 10,000 times and count. That counting approach is essentially what this app does, except instead of a die, it's simulating the Philippine economy.

02 — Every time you hit Run Simulation, the app plays out 10,000 different versions of the next few months. In each version, things unfold slightly differently — oil prices fluctuate, the peso responds differently, businesses react at different speeds — because in real life, no two months are exactly the same. Some versions are mild. Some are bad. Some are catastrophic.

03 — After running all 10,000 versions, the app counts: in how many of these did the economy hit a critical danger point? That count, expressed as a percentage, is the Collapse Probability — the big number you see after running the simulation.

Important: This is not a prediction. A 60% collapse probability doesn't mean "the economy will collapse." It means "under these oil price conditions, 6 out of every 10 possible futures we simulated ended badly." Just like a 60% chance of rain doesn't mean it will rain — but it means you'd be smart to bring an umbrella.

How to use this simulator — step by step
01
SET THE OIL PRICE
Drag the slider to pick a crude oil price in US dollars per barrel. Today it's around $115. Try dragging it to $150 or $180 to simulate what happens if the war escalates and global oil supply gets disrupted further. The higher you drag it, the worse conditions get in the simulation.
02
CHOOSE A TIME HORIZON
How far into the future do you want to simulate? 3 months shows the immediate shock. 6 months shows the medium-term damage as prices stay high. 12 months shows what happens if nothing improves for a full year. Longer horizons generally produce higher collapse probabilities because problems compound over time.
03
SET HOW UNPREDICTABLE THINGS ARE
Low means things go more or less as expected — the economy responds to oil prices in a steady, predictable way. High means wild swings are possible in either direction — a sudden ceasefire could save things, or a Strait of Hormuz closure could make it far worse. High volatility increases risk even at lower oil prices.
04
HIT RUN AND READ THE RESULT
The app runs 10,000 scenarios in seconds. A large number flashes on screen — that's your collapse probability. Then scroll down to see the full breakdown: which warning signs were triggered most often, how conditions evolved month by month, and a sample of individual scenarios from the 10,000.
What do the results actually mean?
📊
THE BIG PERCENTAGE — COLLAPSE PROBABILITY
This is the main result. It tells you: out of 10,000 simulated futures, this many percent ended in economic collapse. Think of it as a danger level, not a prophecy. Under 20% means conditions are manageable. Above 60% means the majority of possible futures look very bad. The color reflects severity — green is safe, orange is serious, red means most futures are headed for collapse.
🌡️
THE THREE WARNING SIGNS
The simulation watches three things that economists use to measure economic health. Inflation — are prices rising too fast? Peso/USD rate — is the peso getting too weak against the dollar? GDP growth — is the economy growing, or shrinking? Each card shows the average outcome across all 10,000 scenarios, and a colored dot (green/orange/red) shows whether that average is in the safe zone or past the danger line.
📈
THE MONTH-BY-MONTH CHART
This chart shows how each warning sign changes over time across your chosen period. The bright line is the most likely path. The shaded area around it is the range of outcomes — from the 10% best-case scenarios to the 10% worst-case ones. The dashed red line is the danger threshold. When the shaded area starts crossing that red line, many of the simulated futures are entering collapse territory.
📉
THE SPREAD CHARTS
These bar charts show how spread out the results were across all 10,000 scenarios. Tall bars bunched together = most futures had similar outcomes. Bars spread wide = outcomes varied wildly. The red bars on the right (or left, for GDP) are the scenarios that crossed into danger territory — more red means more simulated futures hit that threshold.
🗂️
THE SCENARIO TABLE
This shows 20 individual futures randomly picked from the 10,000. Each row is one possible version of the next few months: what oil ended up costing, what inflation did, how the peso moved, whether the economy grew. COLLAPSE = 2 or more danger signs triggered at once. WARNING = 1 danger sign triggered. STABLE = none triggered.
⚠️
WHAT "COLLAPSE" MEANS HERE
Not the end of the Philippines. In this simulator, "collapse" means the economy has crossed a critical combined stress level — measured by a weighted score across all three warning signs. Inflation carries the most weight (40%) because it hits ordinary Filipinos fastest. The peso carries 35% because a weak peso makes every import — including the oil itself — even more expensive. GDP carries 25% because economic shrinkage, while serious, takes longer to be felt. When the combined score crosses the danger threshold, that scenario is counted as a collapse — even if no single indicator is catastrophically bad on its own.
Terms you might not know — explained simply
CRUDE OIL (BRENT)
The raw, unrefined oil that gets pumped out of the ground and sold internationally. "Brent" is the global price benchmark, set in the North Sea. All fuel you buy — gasoline, diesel, kerosene — starts as crude oil. When the crude price goes up, everything downstream goes up too.
MONTE CARLO SIMULATION
A method of figuring out how likely something is by running thousands of random trials instead of trying to calculate it exactly. Named after the famous casino in Monaco — because both involve randomness and probability. Your weather app uses a version of this. So do airplane safety engineers and financial risk analysts.
INFLATION RATE
How fast the prices of everyday things are going up, expressed as a percentage per year. At 4%, your ₱100 grocery basket costs ₱104 next year — manageable. At 10%, it costs ₱110, and salaries rarely rise that fast. The BSP (our central bank) considers anything above 4% as already a concern.
PESO / USD RATE
How many pesos it takes to buy one US dollar. Right now it's around ₱57–58. If it hits ₱80, the peso has become much weaker — meaning everything the Philippines imports (including oil, medicine, electronics) suddenly costs a lot more in peso terms, even if the dollar price stayed the same.
GDP GROWTH
GDP (Gross Domestic Product) is the total value of everything the Philippines produces in a year — goods, services, everything. Growth means the economy got bigger. Zero or negative means it stopped growing or shrank. When GDP shrinks, companies earn less, hire fewer people, and some close down entirely.
VOLATILITY
How unpredictable and unstable a situation is. Low volatility = things are relatively steady and outcomes are close to what you'd expect. High volatility = anything can happen — things could get much better or much worse than expected. Wars, sanctions, and supply shocks all increase volatility.
STRAIT OF HORMUZ
A narrow waterway — only 33 kilometers wide at its narrowest — between Iran and Oman. About 20% of all the oil in the world passes through it every day. If this strait closes or becomes dangerous to navigate due to the war, global oil prices spike almost immediately, and the Philippines feels it within weeks.
PROBABILITY
A number between 0% and 100% that expresses how likely something is. 0% = impossible. 100% = certain. 50% = as likely as a coin flip. In this app, the collapse probability tells you how likely economic collapse is under the conditions you set — not whether it will definitely happen.
⚠
This is an educational tool, not a crystal ball. The simulation is built on real published data from the BSP, PSA, MUFG Research, and the U.S. Energy Information Administration — but it is deliberately simplified. The real economy is shaped by government decisions, international negotiations, consumer behavior, and countless other factors this model does not capture. The collapse probability shown is a risk estimate under specific assumptions — not a forecast of what will happen. Use it to understand risk, not to predict the future.

Variables are correlated (ρinf,peso = 0.72 · ρinf,gdp = −0.65 · ρpeso,gdp = −0.58) via Cholesky decomposition. Collapse triggers when the weighted stress index (inflation×0.45, peso×0.35, GDP×0.20) reaches ≥ 1.00, OR when inflation alone reaches ≥ 10.5%. The standalone inflation rule exists because in the Philippines, fuel-driven inflation above 10% is not just one economic indicator among many — it is an immediate household emergency. Oil price increases pass through directly and simultaneously into jeepney and trucking fares, electricity generation costs, food prices, and manufacturing inputs. Unlike in more diversified economies, there is no sector that is insulated. A Filipino family does not need the peso to collapse or GDP to contract to feel the full weight of 10–11% inflation. They feel it the next time they commute, buy rice, or pay their electric bill.
